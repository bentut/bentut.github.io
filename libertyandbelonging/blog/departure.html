Departure

7/5/2019

0 Comments

 
“I’m actually at the airport right now!”

“Oh yeah? Where are you headed?”

“Well... (sheepishly) I’m going on a trip as part of my omidyar fellowship. My idea is to go to Louisiana to study race and place in America, you know.. the legacy of slavery and crimes against indigenous peoples,u crimes of America. You know, the usual...”

“Well it’s really appropriate that you are diving right into the heart of this since it’s the 4th of July.”

“YES!! [much more excited and less sheepish] Thank you!!You have no idea how validating that is.
[Overhead speaker while In my seat on the airplane, speaking with a strong foreign accent]
“Due to the time difference, it’s 12:35 in Houston time where we are headed, and officially July 4th. Happy 4th of July!”
I consider myself the kind of person that needs a lot of validation. At the same time, I do a lot of things that other people consider strange, so I’m not sure thst it’s a characteristic that others really see in me or would agree that I have. My experience of it is of feeling extremely exposed, uncomfortable and awkward, in situations where I feel like I have not received an explicit invitation, confirmation, or affirmation. At least in situations where it really feels like it matters.
​
Discussing race, particularly the experience of being black, feels like one of those places to me. I’m brown - a Mexican filipino hapa, but also privileged... the son of two doctors (even if they are immigrants and first generation college grads) and a Stanford grad (something I’m both proud of and ashamed of at the same time) . The experience of being black in America is something I have no direct experience with and I question the validity of my participation in any discussion about it. At the same time, it’s a discussion that triggers me, that I am drawn to, and that i feel, for many reasons, is vital to the future of our nation, and how we govern, two things that I am definitely involved in.

I’m going to Louisiana to understand how I can be a part of fighting to fix everything that is wrong with how we treat people as Americans, how we treat our black neighbors, how we treat our indigenous forerunners — ultimately how we treat each other. In Louisiana — a place I’ve only visited once as an adult, and seemingly a world away from Hawaii — the kind of work we can be doing on these fronts is on full display. a valued guide and new friend drawn there by her ancestral roots and thesis in indigenous journalism has pointed out many people and places to connect to in the various places of louisiana. I go in trying to follow leads she has provided and the people she has connect me to. Volunteering - the way I articulated in my proposal, seems like a stretch, given how little time I am spending there. Even finding people to meet with seems ambitious to some extent given the timing of the trip. Scheduling the first couple of days on a potentially 4 day weekend could be considered by some “bad planning” — and another reason the validation is so valuable. But experiencing and learning things is something I will figure out how to do.

I do want to share something that my friend Kim brought up when I told her my intention for planning this trip when I did: Frederick douglass’s Comments about the 4th of July. As read by James Earl Jones... and full text below.
Fellow-citizens, pardon me, allow me to ask, why am I called upon to speak here to-day? What have I, or those I represent, to do with your national independence? Are the great principles of political freedom and of natural justice, embodied in that Declaration of Independence, extended to us? And am I, therefore, called upon to bring our humble offering to the national altar, and to confess the benefits and express devout gratitude for the blessings resulting from your independence to us?

I am not included within the pale of this glorious anniversary! Your high independence only reveals the immeasurable distance between us. The blessings in which you this day rejoice are not enjoyed in common. The rich inheritance of justice, liberty, prosperity, and independence bequeathed by your fathers is shared by you, not by me. The sunlight that brought life and healing to you has brought stripes and death to me. This Fourth of July is yours, not mine. You may rejoice, I must mourn. To drag a man in fetters into the grand illuminated temple of liberty, and call upon him to join you in joyous anthems, were inhuman mockery and sacrilegious irony. Do you mean, citizens, to mock me, by asking me to speak today?

What, to the American slave, is your Fourth of July? I answer: a day that reveals to him, more than all other days of the year, the gross injustice and cruelty to which he is a constant victim. To him, your celebration is a sham; your boasted liberty, an unholy license; your national greatness, swelling vanity; your sounds of rejoicing are empty and heartless; your denunciation of tyrants, brass fronted impudence; your shouts of liberty and equality, hollow mockery; your prayers and hymns, your sermons and thanksgivings, with all your religious parade and solemnity, are, to Him, mere bombast, fraud, deception, impiety, and hypocrisy—a thin veil to cover up crimes that would disgrace a nation of savages. There is not a nation of the earth guilty of practices more shocking and bloody than are the people of these United States at this very hour.
At a time like this, scorching irony, not convincing argument, is needed. O! had I the ability, and could reach the nation’s ear, I would, to-day, pour forth a stream, a fiery stream of biting ridicule, blasting reproach, withering sarcasm, and stern rebuke. For it is not light that is needed, but fire; it is not the gentle shower, but thunder. We need the storm, the whirlwind, the earthquake. The feeling of the nation must be quickened; the conscience of the nation must be roused; the propriety of the nation must be startled; the hypocrisy of the nation must be exposed; and the crimes against God and man must be proclaimed and denounced.
