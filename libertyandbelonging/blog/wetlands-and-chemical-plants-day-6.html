Wetlands and Chemical Plants (Day 6)

7/12/2019

0 Comments

 
I ended the day at the Vacherie Community Center, coincidentally, right in front of a sign pointing down the street to the Whitney Plantation we visite the day before. Gathered outside were a group of people wearing matching yellow shirts reading, “Rise St James”, talking into loudspeakers and holding Signs that read “no jobs on a dead planet” and “we stand with students worldwide #climatestrike”. The Louisiana Department of Environmental Quality was holding a public meeting about Formosa Chemical’s application for an air quality permit. The St. James community is located in what is increasingly known nationally as Cancer Alley, where residents, particularly black communities, suffer increased instances of cancer while elevated levels of toxic pollutants are discharged by the many chemical plants currently situated there along the Mississippi River between Baton Rouge and New Orleans.
But I started the day on a solo excursion to Lousiana’s Bayou Country in Houma. I went to Houma to catch Jonathan Foret, the Executive Director of the South Louisiana wetland discovery center before his late lunch. Jonathan, along with Jessi Parfait was one of two people that I had gotten in contact with before I left Honolulu. Both were connected to so many vital issues, organizations, and work areas, that I wanted to ensure I’d have time on the later end of my trip to follow any leads they pointed out. Jessi, had for example, directed me to both the LDEQ hearing above and the New Orleans People’s assembly meeting that I had hoped to join a few days later on Thursday night.

While Jonathan and I were sitting in his office chatting, he got news that the funding for the first phase of construction of the discovery center was on the agenda for the bond commission, a critical step that might have them breaking ground and starting construction by the end of the year. Having lived through state funding processes and currently living through a monster construction project I was really happy for him and for SWLDC!

We didn’t have too much time to chat, but I had lots of questions about the Bayou and the wetlands and Jonathan agreed to take me out on a driving tour of the Bayou a few days later on Thursday. Part of the thinking was that it looked like there was a storm brewing on the gulf and the tour might be more viable Thursday than Friday.

Jonathan’s lunch was with a woman who helped create and administer a program called LASAFE which trained and developed leaders already embedded and respected in community to facilitate community conversations. She was another person I wanted to meet very much, but I was committed to a 2:00 back in New Orleans to have a chat with Mama Denise Graves of the Congo Square Preservation Society.

My meeting with Mama Denise was full of incredible stories of doing grassroots organizing, building power in communities, subverting and leveraging institutional positions, and personal challenges to find and articulate my values. It was that last one that hit home especially for me. I have always considered myself a principled person... but at that moment, (and in this one), I could not tell you what my values are or why I don’t have that answer.

Mama shared a bit about her own ongoing spiritual journey and study of various forms of spirituality, healing, and tradition. She was on her way to England and Ireland to explore Celtic traditions and had been trying to make her way to Vietnam as well. She reminded me of Nana Veary in the diversity of her pursuit, despite her clear mastery of many aspects of spirituality (Mama Denise lead an incredible and fluent offering of libations to ancestors during the Congo Square drumming circle).

Mama shared about her 2 biological and 5 non biological children. Some of the stories were sad, all of them were remarkable. Reflections on parenthood and having kids has been a theme of this trip. I am understanding the relationship between values, children, and humanness in ways I never did previously.

Right afterwards I headed up to Vacherie for the community meeting. I got there pretty early, and figured something was up when I saw photographers, two sheriffs trucks and armed officers (one with a beautifully detailed tattoo sleeve of feathers and fur - who seemed to appreciate the compliment when I told her I thought so). I got there at the same time as two conspicuously dressed gentlemen wearing sports coats and slacks, looking a bit like my stereotype of souther plantation owners. They never spoke in the meeting, but shook a lot of hands and received a few snarky comments from one of the lead activists.

Jessi warned me that I might be disgusted with how the meeting was run. As a city employee, having been a part of many public meetings where the community was angry at my agency, I thought I might understand where the LDEQ was coming from. Jessi was right.

It was an upsetting display of an almost entirely black community all, unanimously expressing their strong rejection of this project and their anger and hurt about how their community has suffered. Meanwhile, every testimonial in support of the project came from a person that was white and that did not live in the community — including the Parish Concilmember our president, I forget which, who was ostensible representing the community. The testifies were passionate and moving and honest and proud and resilient. They inspired support from students around the country that came to that meeting there in Vacherie. The final decision was not going to be made at that meeting, but I suspect that the resistance was not going to be enough.
