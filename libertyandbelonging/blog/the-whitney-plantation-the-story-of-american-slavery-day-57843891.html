The Whitney Plantation: The Story of American Slavery (Day 5)

7/12/2019

0 Comments

 
Click on image above to see more photos
A few days ago while we were taking a rest at Essence Fest, Kimberly brought out a pamphlet advertising the Whitney Plantation. She had heard about it previously and had been interested in going for a while. I had not heard of it, nor had I really contemplated going on any tours as part of this excursion, but all of the accounts of the Whitney Plantation indicated it was an important experience. Given that I am here in Louisiana, in large part to better understand the legacy of slavery as part of the US, it was a gift dropped from heaven.
An even bigger gift was Sabrina and Luana coming with me. As we noted afterward, it was not an experience that one really wants to have alone. I was really grateful to have friends with me at the plantation, and someone to talk about it afterward.

The Whitney Plantation was a sugar cane plantation. Sugar was unique in the plantation agricultural worlds because of a 3 month window during which all of the harvesting and importantly all of the production needed to be done. Because cane juice spoils, the sugar had to be processed immediately, on site, which made the plantations, factories as much as they were farms. Enslaved people worked around clock to maximize the production of sugar. The Union Pacific railroad picked it up directly from the plantation. It was notorious for being one of the worst types of work. In one year the Whitney plantation by itself produced 25% of the country’s sugar.

The stories that make up the Whitney’s foundation are oral accounts from formerly enslaved people. The stories were collected during the works progress administration era by writers (funded by the federal government in the new deal) who realized what would be lost if the last surviving people who had experienced America plantation slavery were not able to share their personal accounts.

The plantation was a collection of both restored structures, not unlike Hawaii’s own plantation villages, and solemn and beautiful memorials to the men, women, children who had worked and often died on the plantation, as well as the children and infants born into that life that never made it to adulthood.

It’s difficult to capture the shame and sadness that come with confronting American slavery on the site and in the buildings where the suffering took place. A trip that we decided not to take, but might have had the same potential — with similar perspective of firsthand accounts — was to the Angola prison, known for many years as America’s worst prison. As another Louisiana friend, Jessi Parfait, the archivist for the United Houma Nation shared with me, the prison is sited on what was one a beautiful Houma settlement.

Without drawing away from the shamefulness of the violence our society inflicted on its people, the three of us were in awe of what our ancestors, created, lived through and passed forward. As Sabrina pointed out, even with all that happened, “we’re still here.” That makes me really proud, even though the people who were enslaved at the Whitney were not my direct biological ancestors. I hope that they would be proud of the work we do and the lives we lead today.
