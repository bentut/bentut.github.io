Questions

7/5/2019

0 Comments

 
I was advised by Chris block, my “learning Sherpa”, that I would want to go into this journey with a few questions. Questions that I would ask the people I encountered. But more importantly questions that I would use to check in with myself. After some discussion, we settled on a few.
  • What am I afraid of?
  • What are others afraid of?
  • What are my stories?
On the flight over, I encountered a few more.
My typical solo travel (say to a work conference) is pretty predictable. I had no reason to believe this flight to MSY would be any different. In fact, I had just done this exact same route last April — incidentally the trip where I applied for my inclusion in the 6th cohort of the omidyar fellows, I did not guess that I would be retracing those steps for my ILE just over a year later.

I was tired from “cramming” for this trip over the next few nights, but the passenger next to me was a chatty flight attendant uncomfortable in his seat and eager for the doors to close so he could jump into another one. I figured it was time to kick this life changing adventure off by doing things differently than I normally would, so I engaged in conversation where I felt myself wanting to withdraw.
Where are you off to?

Louisiana.

What's in Louisiana?

I'm not sure. But I’m doing this trip as part of a leadership development fellowship I’m a part of.

Interesting. That’s cool. So you're being developed into a leader. Do you feel like a leader?
Good question, new friend. I said I did and asked him the same thing. He said he did, too. Later, when passengers around us were screaming because the girl in the row behind us was having a seizure, he showed how true that was by leaping over our aisle neighbor and helping the people get the girl turned to her side before jumping back into his seat before I could even figure out what was going on.

Another thing I admired about new friend leader was how easily he struck up conversation with everyone around him, in spite of seeming both stressed, impatient, and generally fed up with life. One of these people was the quiet gentleman sitting in the aisle seat of our row. He was en route to New Orleans also... for Essence Fest.

I had had heard about Essence Fest from my friend Imani who connected me with all of the people Louisiana that she had interviewed for her thesis. She mentioned another friend was going — doing a fascinating and related project on decolonizing and to try to connect with her. Essence Fest, I found out was the annual festival for Essence Magazine, a black women’s magazine. If you’ve seen the film Girls Trip, that’s about a trip to essence fest. If you’ve ever been to SXSW, that’s a good reference for scale and diversity of content as well as corporate sponsorship.

​Because I had planne the earlier part of my trip during a holiday, many of the contact I had hoped to connect with were unavailable. Essence fest seemed like it would be a great way to connect with the experience of being black in America, and sitting in a row with someone (and probably a whole plane full of people ) going, seemed to be a confirmation of that. I asked our aisle neighbor what it was like, what I shouldn’t miss, and he described what he liked to do and what it was like.

Essence fest and it’s tremendous gravity turned out to be a huge factor the rest of my travel to my first stop in New Orleans. In Houston, the gate agents were desperately trying to find volunteers to give up their spot on the oversold flight to fly the next day. They were offering obscene amounts of money. No one was budging, presumably because of essence fest. I decided i could wait to get to Louisiana and let someone else make it over to what they were trying to get to. The gate area was also full of passengers flying standby to get to essence fest.

Whe all was said and done, giving up my seat got one more person to NOLA, (none of the standby passengers made it on. They rerouted to Lafayette and planned to take a car from there). The flight they were going to put me on was a 7AM to MSY the next day, but they also had a 2PM to Baton Rouge if I was interested (which I was). I took that flight to Baton Rouge making friends with another passenger also going to essence fest (also volunteering to take a different flight for a voucher), sharing an hour long cab ride with my new friend, his wife, and his wife’s cousin (and our cab driver who had been driving for 40years and once driven a cab from Baton Rouge to Detroit Michigan), learning about their annual pilgrimage to essence fest, their parents and kids, care taking and taxes, NFL picks and NBA allegiances. They were wonderful, and highly understanding of my somewhat strange situation. I feel like I got a lifetimes worth of life advice on family and responsibility. To begin to reciprocate I offered to use some of my newfound flight wealth to secure them one of their tickets to their family reunion.

But I want to rewind a bit and come back to questions. Rather than sleep on the red eye to Houston, I opted to watch a few movies. One of the films (I believe a recommendation from my brother in film and our very own omidyar fellow program director Bill Coy) was “Tales of Bill Murray”, a story of all of the spontaneous and unexpected things that he does that turn up in urban legends... washing dishes at keggers, tending bar at random bars, showing up in random karaoke rooms. The takeaway was that Bill Murray gives a bit of himself every he intervenes. Because it matters. He’s never afraid that he’ll look stupid, that he’ll lose status, that he’ll lose opportunities... he engages with individuals and is present in that moment because that is what life is about. He shows people how to live and he is an embodiment of love and of aloha. And thus, my final guiding question: what would bill Murray do?
