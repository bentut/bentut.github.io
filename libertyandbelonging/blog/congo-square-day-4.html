Congo Square (Day 4)

7/7/2019

0 Comments

 
Click the image to see all photos
A few weekends ago, my friend Kevin at KVIBE called me up and said the boys were going to Mokauea island to do a reef cleanup and asked if Tori and I wanted to go. I had wanted to visit KVIBE and sit in on one of their circles as preparation for this trip. I was not able to do that, but I realize now that what I needed to bring with me came from that experience at Mauli Ola and Mokauea. One of the many things that I learned that day are that Hawaiian heiau (sacred alters) are not structures, but sites. It is the site that is sacred, not what is on the site. So they can’t be moved. Moreover, all of the heiau are connected. Congo Square, is in my opinion, one of the most sacred heiau in the world, just not visited primarily by hawaiians and not on the hawaiian islands.
Congo Square is in Louis Armstrong park in the Treme neighborhood of New Orleans. Treme is recognized as America’s oldest black neighborhood. The history of Congo Square is fascinating, rich, and of great importance. My description will not do it justice and I encourage everyone reading this to learn as much as you can about it. It is on located at a site known to be sacred to the Houma tribe of indigenous people and during the times when it received the name Congo Square, it was a place where enslaved people were allowed to gather unsupervised and participate in their cultural practices from Africa. The cultural celebration it enabled can, as Wynton Marsalis points out, be traced forward into every form of American music today.

the Congo Square preservation society convenes drumming circles every Sunday afternoon. It was one of the things I was most eager to experience, and I wasn’t sure exactly when it would happen when I first arrived in New Orleans. I stayed in a hotel in Treme just to be nearby. Luckily, we ran into Mama Denise of the Congo Square Preservation Society at the conclusion of the Maafa on the previous day and she was able to tell us the exact time.

I arrived in Congo Square a little early to essentially an empty park. After waiting about 30 minutes after it was supposed to start, I began to lose hope about it happening. The a truck drove up onto the stones, and another one pulled up right behind it. People started setting up little chairs, and I just about ran over to the other side of the square where they were.

As I type this, my palms still feel tender from where they hit the rims of the drums, a few hundred (maybe a thousand?) times. They got especially tender after I started hitting the drum for real - after having it taken away and getting called out for faking it. When I arrived, the sun was high in the sky and the whole day was ahead of me. By the time I left after chatting with people and taking some photos, a guard on a golf cart had to whistle at me and tell me to leave because the park was closing.

To to say that drumming with the Congo preservation society was a spiritual experience does not capture to me in my own mind what happened to me there. Or maybe it just elevates my understanding of what a spiritual experience really is. Between dancing and drumming, thinking and feeling, I connected to something powerful. I wasn’t always connected to it, in fact, it was probably only a tiny fraction of my time there. But I got there a few times, and got a taste for who I needed to be to find it.

​At at one point after my first drum had been withdrawn and the circle was really getting going, Mama Denise had me move into the middle of the circle and dance. I had been a part of the circle the entire time, even when I was not drumming. So I was always close to it. But on the perimeter. At that particular time, feeling the middle of the circle (was it also responding to me dancing?) was a completely different thing at a completely different level. Looking back at it now, feeling that moment over again, I was connected, like a beam of light through the middle of the earth to all of the heiau in Hawaii. I was scared to stay in it too long, afraid of taking up too much space. But I wish I had been there longer.
